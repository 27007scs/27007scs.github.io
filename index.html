AI laws should protect people first

We need AI rules with real accountability, not vague promises.

Artificial intelligence can help society, but without clear laws it can also scale bias, surveillance, misinformation, and unchecked corporate power. Public policy should set boundaries before harm becomes normal.

What smart AI law should guarantee

  • Clear disclosure when AI is making or shaping important decisions
  • Independent audits for high-risk systems
  • Human appeal rights when people are harmed
  • Real penalties when companies break the rules
100% transparency for high-risk AI
0 tolerance for secret algorithmic harm
1 shared standard: people over profit

Five core demands

Good AI laws should be practical, enforceable, and human-centered.

Transparency

People deserve to know when AI is being used in hiring, lending, healthcare, education, policing, and public services.

Safety Testing

Powerful systems should be tested before deployment, not after public damage has already occurred.

Bias Protections

Laws must require evidence that AI tools do not discriminate against protected groups or vulnerable communities.

Human Oversight

Automated decisions should never trap people without meaningful review, explanation, and a path to appeal.

Enforcement

A law without audits, fines, and public reporting is just a press release. Accountability needs real consequences.

Voluntary promises are not enough

Responsible AI policy is pro-innovation because it builds trust.

FAQ

Common questions about AI law and public accountability.

Are AI laws anti-technology?

No. Good laws create standards so innovation does not depend on secrecy, exploitation, or avoidable harm.

Why not rely on company self-regulation?

Self-regulation often lacks independent checks, meaningful transparency, and enforceable consequences.

What counts as high-risk AI?

Systems used in areas like employment, healthcare, education, finance, housing, law enforcement, and government services should receive the strongest oversight.
